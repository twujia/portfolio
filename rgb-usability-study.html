Case Study

RBG Website Usability Study

Improving planning confidence for visitors by identifying usability breakdowns and producing practical, evidence-based recommendations.

Role
UX Researcher
Date
Feb - Jun 2025
Scope
Mixed-method usability study
Skills
Usability Testing, User Research, Information Architecture, Research Synthesis

TL;DR

Problem: The Royal Botanic Gardens Victoria website presented significant usability barriers for general visitors, with task success rates falling well below the industry benchmark of 80% across both moderated and remote testing.

Process: We conducted a mixed-method usability study combining moderated in-person testing with 6 participants and remote tree testing via UsabiliTree with 17 participants.

Outcome: The website revealed 5 key findings around ambiguous navigation labels, missing features, insufficient service information, poor visibility, and unintuitive information placement. Each issue was paired with actionable design recommendations.

Project Context

  • The Royal Botanic Gardens (RBG) website is the primary planning touchpoint for anyone looking to visit, book events, access services, or explore the gardens.
  • Despite the breadth of content the website hosts, no systematic usability evaluation had been conducted for general visitors, who are the largest user segment.
  • Given how heavily website usability influences whether people successfully plan and follow through on a visit, this was a gap worth investigating.

The Problem

The website failed to support common visitor planning tasks clearly and efficiently, especially when users needed specific service details, navigational certainty, or confidence in where critical information would appear.

Results

  • The website failed to meet the effectiveness benchmark across both moderated testing (69%) and remote testing (56.3%), where both were below the 80% target.
  • Efficiency was partially met; remote testing averaged 24 seconds per task (within benchmark), while moderated testing averaged 76.5 seconds.
  • The SUS score of 71.7 exceeded the 68 threshold, though the average SEQ of 5.2 fell short of the 5.5 target, indicating users found tasks generally manageable but not effortless.

Recommendations

Recommendation #1: Renaming ambiguous navigation labels

Replace unclear labels with terms that match visitor mental models and intent.

Recommendation #2: Adding sorting and filtering tools to content-heavy pages

Improve findability for events, spaces, and map-heavy content with filter/search support.

"I would like to see a list of event in a single place or a list of spaces in a single place that have a short filtering searching feature... And also for the live interaction map, I would like to see a search keyword text input there."

"That's a lot of gardens...They are not alphabetically sorted...So I just had to read every single label to navigate through it. Took quite a while."

Recommendation #3: Providing more supportive information

Add practical planning details directly where users make decisions.

"...I can only book it for up to three hours... if I need to visit for, like, five hours, then I would need to contact them or, like, do something differently.."

"There's not really any picnic information... I can't really find a way to like, see if there's a way I can do a picnic or not in the garden... but I don't know if like, I am permitted or not to do a picnic there."

Recommendation #4: Surfacing accessibility and service information more prominent

Make accessibility and service details visible at key navigation and page entry points.

"There's just one thing about when I get into the map is that that hyperlink of the map, it's quite small and easy to miss if I don't like look clearly."

"I would expect it to be on that gray card in here... I would expect some sort of accessibility information on this card."

Recommendation #5: Adding filming and family-friendly picnic information directly to relevant garden pages

Surface policy and family-planning details on the specific pages where users expect them.

Outcomes and Reflections

This study showed that usability barriers were less about content volume and more about content structure, label clarity, and information placement. Combining moderated and remote methods helped validate this from both behavioral and structural perspectives.

Reflection: translating findings into explicit recommendations improved handoff quality and made the work immediately actionable for design and content stakeholders.
